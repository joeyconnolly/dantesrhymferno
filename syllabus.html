Curricular Architecture: The Path of Poetic Inquiry

A carefully structured journey through twelve dimensions of poetic craft, each building upon the foundations established in previous sessions. This evolving framework mirrors the recursive nature of poetic thinking itself—where each new insight necessitates the revisiting and deepening of fundamental principles.

Overview

This twelve-week course traverses the multifaceted landscape of poetic expression, from foundational elements of imagery and sound to advanced explorations of intertextuality and linguistic limits. Each module combines theoretical understanding with practical application, fostering a dialectical relationship between analysis and creation.

Week 1: The Poetic Image – Beyond Visual Representation

This inaugural module interrogates the nature of imagery in poetry, moving beyond reductive conceptions of description to explore how images function as epistemological instruments—ways of knowing and experiencing reality that transcend simple visual representation. We examine how poets transform phenomenological encounters into complex symbolic frameworks that simultaneously engage the senses and intellect.

Essential Voices

  • Wallace Stevens: Philosophical meditations on perception and reality
  • Sylvia Plath: Visceral transformations of the self through imagistic intensity
  • Octavio Paz: Existential cartographies of interior landscapes

Textual Exploration: "The Idea of Order at Key West" by Wallace Stevens

Stevens' meditation on artistic creation presents a profound philosophical inquiry into the relationship between human consciousness and external reality. The poem's central tension—between the raw, indifferent power of the ocean and the woman's transformative song—becomes a metapoetic commentary on how art imposes meaningful patterns upon chaotic experience.

Wallace Stevens (1879–1955) was an American modernist whose work consistently explores the nature of reality and imagination, positioning poetry as a supreme fiction that both acknowledges and transcends the limitations of human understanding.

Textual Exploration: "Ariel" by Sylvia Plath

In this kinetic, transformative work, Plath presents a complex psychodrama of identity dissolution and reconstruction through intensely physical imagery. The poem functions as a phenomenological record of consciousness in flux, where the boundaries between self and world become increasingly permeable.

Sylvia Plath (1932–1963) revolutionized confessional poetry through her unflinching examination of psychological states and her distinctive fusion of mythic symbolism with intensely personal experience.

Textual Exploration: "The Street" by Octavio Paz

Paz constructs a minimalist yet profound meditation on existential quest, using the image of an endless street as both literal setting and metaphysical framework. The poem's sparse architecture belies its philosophical complexity—the persistent pursuit of a shadow that remains perpetually beyond reach becomes an allegory for consciousness itself.

Octavio Paz (1914–1998), Mexican poet and Nobel laureate, consistently explored the intersections of time, language, and being throughout his work, creating a distinctive poetics that bridged Western and non-Western philosophical traditions.

Creative Laboratories

1. Transfigurative Imagery

Objective: Transubstantiate a commonplace object through multisensory imagery, revealing its latent symbolic potential.

Methodology:

  • Select a quotidian object from your immediate environment
  • Develop a sensory cartography that engages at least three non-visual perceptual modes
  • Gradually transmute the object's concrete presence into a vehicle for abstract or universal significance
  • Employ indirection rather than declaration—allow the juxtaposition of images to generate meaning without explicit statement

Catalyst: Transform an ordinary timepiece into an emblem of mortality or transcendence, using auditory and tactile imagery as primary vehicles.

2. Ekphrastic Transfiguration

Objective: Apprehend the essential qualities of an abstract artwork without resorting to descriptive enumeration.

Methodology:

  • Identify an abstract composition that resonates with your aesthetic sensibilities
  • Articulate your phenomenological response rather than cataloging visual elements
  • Develop metaphorical correspondences that transpose visual experience into broader existential contexts
  • Employ rhythmic and structural elements that mirror the compositional dynamics of the artwork

Catalyst: Create a poetic response to Kandinsky's "Composition VIII" that explores the dialectical relationship between chaos and order through parallel linguistic structures.

Your Response

Dialogic Inquiries

1. Transcendent Imagery: How do the assigned poets transcend referential or descriptive imagery to create what Gaston Bachelard might call "poetic space"? Identify specific textual moments where images function simultaneously as sensory experience and conceptual architecture.

2. Productive Ambiguity: Consider how imprecision and indeterminacy function as generative forces within poetic imagery. How might the deliberate cultivation of ambiguity create a more participatory relationship between reader and text?

3. Epistemological Function: Explore how imagery in one of the assigned poems serves not merely as decoration or illustration, but as a mode of knowing—a way of apprehending aspects of experience that resist conceptual articulation.

Week 2: The Music of Language – Sound as Meaning

This module examines how sound in poetry functions not merely as decorative accompaniment to semantic content, but as a fundamental bearer of meaning itself. We explore how phonetic patterning, rhythmic structures, and sonic textures create embodied experiences that complement, complicate, or transcend the denotative dimensions of language.

Essential Voices

  • Gerard Manley Hopkins: Revolutionary metrical innovations and sonic density
  • Langston Hughes: Musical transpositions and vernacular cadences
  • Seamus Heaney: Tactile sound-embodiments of cultural memory

Textual Exploration: "The Windhover" by Gerard Manley Hopkins

Hopkins' revolutionary "sprung rhythm" and dense phonetic patterning create a sonic experience that mirrors the kinetic energy of the falcon's flight. Through intricate alliteration, assonance, and consonance, he achieves a compression of sound that embodies rather than merely describes his subject, creating what he called "inscape"—the unique interior essence of a thing made perceptible.

Gerard Manley Hopkins (1844–1889) pioneered a revolutionary approach to poetic rhythm and sound, developing "sprung rhythm" to capture the dynamic energies of natural phenomena and spiritual experience.

Creative Laboratories

1. Phonetic Orchestration

Objective: Compose a poem where sound patterns function as primary carriers of meaning.

Methodology:

  • Select a phenomenological experience with distinctive sonic qualities
  • Develop clusters of alliteration, assonance, and consonance that embody rather than describe these qualities
  • Experiment with line length and caesura to control the breath and pacing of sound
  • Create tensions between sound patterns and semantic content that generate productive ambiguities

Catalyst: Compose a poem about rainfall that employs sibilants, plosives, and liquid consonants to create an auditory equivalent of water's various states and movements.

Week 12: The Art of Revision – Refining the Poetic Craft

Our concluding module examines the crucial role of revision in poetic creation, understanding editing not as mere correction but as a deepening of vision—a re-seeing that clarifies and intensifies the original impulse. Through studying poets' manuscripts and multiple versions, we explore how the recursive process of revision embodies a dialogue between initial inspiration and critical consciousness.

Essential Voices

  • Adrienne Rich: Political and personal transformations through revisionary practice
  • W.B. Yeats: The evolution of symbolic language across multiple drafts
  • Donald Hall & Jane Hirshfield: Essays on the poetics of refinement

Textual Exploration: "Diving into the Wreck" by Adrienne Rich

Rich's iconic poem, viewed alongside earlier drafts, reveals how precision emerges through the elimination of the extraneous and the careful calibration of metaphorical registers. The poem's exploration of personal and cultural archaeology parallels the revisionary process itself—a diving into the submerged structures of language to recover essential meaning.

Adrienne Rich (1929–2012) was renowned for her evolving poetic voice, which increasingly aligned poetic form with political consciousness through rigorous revisionary practice.

Creative Laboratories

Radical Revision

Objective: Transform a previous poem through substantive revision that reconsiders its fundamental premises.

Methodology:

  • Select a poem from earlier in the course that feels unresolved or underdeveloped
  • Create multiple versions that experiment with different formal structures, tonal registers, and perspectives
  • Deliberately challenge your initial assumptions about the poem's subject or approach
  • Document your revisionary process, noting pivotal moments of recognition or reconsideration

Catalyst: Revisit your response to the Week 1 transfigurative imagery exercise, reimagining it through the formal and conceptual techniques acquired throughout the course.
